The purpose of this module is to describe how an option contract works, how the payoffs for an option contract is determined and the sensitivity of the option contract's selling price to exogenous changes such as the level of futures price volatility and the number of days until the contract expires. The first section of this module provides a general overview of option contracts. This overview includes a discussion of the associated jargon such as "strike price", "in the money" and "intrinsic value". The second section is devoted to constructing payoff diagrams for options sellers and option buyers. The last section is used to conduct sensitivity analysis using a series of simulated payoffs for a call option and using the calculated values of the marginal impacts which are known as the "Greeks".

(i) Overview of Commodity Options

Stock options which are included in the compensation package of the CEO of a large firm are widely used and are generally well understood. The call option allows the CEO to purchase stock of her company at a predetermined strike price at any time on or before the expiry of the call option contract. If the share price remains below the strike price and the option is set to expire, the CEO will not exercise her option to purchase shares. The call option incentivizes the CEO to work hard because for every dollar increase in the share price above the option's strike price is a dollar of compensation for the CEO. An alternative strategy (seldom used in the real world) is to force the CEO to hold put options. If the CEO performs well and the share price rises above the strike price then the put option will not be exercised. If instead the CEO performs poorly and the stock price falls below the strike price then the board of directors can exercise the put option, which forces the CEO to purchase stock at the current share price and thus take a financial hit.

Exercising a call option on a commodity futures contact gives the holder of the option a long position in the futures market at the option's strike price. If this long position is offset with a short position then the call's payoff is equal to the futures price when the call is exercised minus the strike price. Similarly, exercising a put option on a commodity futures contract gives the holder a short futures position at the option's strike price. If this short position is offset with a long position then the put's payoff is equal to the strike price minus the futures price when the put is exercised. It follows that a call option will be exercised only if the futures price is above the strike price, and a put option will be exercised only if the futures price is below the strike price.


Call Option: Exercising results in a long futures position. When offset the payoff is equal to the futures price minus the strike price.

Put Option: Exercising results in a short futures position. When offset the payoff is equal to the strike price minus the futures price.

Examples from the Corn Market

A futures contact is an obligation to accept delivery or make delivery of the commodity. This obligation can be offset but it cannot be resold to another trader. In contrast, an option contract is initially written/sold by a professional (often a firm) and this contract can be resold and repurchased many times before either the option expires without being exercised or it is exercised on or before the expiry date. For agricultural commodities the CME trades American-style options, which allows the holder to exercise before the option expires. An European-style option can only be exercised at the point that it expires (see Investopedia). There are dozens of call and put options that trade for a commodity such as corn at a given point in time. This is because options have different strike prices and different expiry dates. Figure 2-A-3 demonstrates that out of the money options with zero intrinsic value trade at a positive price due to the option's positive time value. The concept of time value is explored in greated deal in the next section but for now it is sufficient to interpret time value as the expected payout given the probability that the futures price will favourably change (e.g., increase for a call and decrease for a put). For an option that is currently in the money, the intrinsic value is the payout if the option was exercised immediately, and the time value is the residual (i.e., the price of the option minus its intrinsic value). Intrinsic value: The difference between the futures price and the strike price of an in the money call, and the difference between the strike price and the futures price for an in the money put. The Intrinsic value is zero for out of the money options.

Time value: The price of the option minus its intrinsic value.

(ii) Payoff Diagrams

The firm which writes (i.e., sells the original) call or put option collects the original selling price and in exchange is responsible for making payment to a firm or individual who may eventually exercise the option. There is no limit as to how much the futures price can change (other than remaining non-negative) and for this reason the payment from the option writer to the option exerciser can potentially be very large. In contrast, the most that can be lost by the person or firm which holds the option contract is the original cost of buying the option. The limited loss feature of an option contract makes them suitable to be held by individual investors in the absence of a margin account.

Payoff diagrams for European-style options are an effective way to show the potential gains and losses from selling or buying call or put options. Figure 2-A-4 shows a payoff diagram for the case of buyer of a call option. The horizontal axis is the futures price when the call option is expiring. If the futures price is below the strike price (left side of diagram) then the out of the money call option has zero intrinsic value and it will not be exercised by its holder. In this case the payoff to the option holder is a loss equal to the purchase price of the option. If instead the futures price is above the strike price (right side of diagram) then the in the money call option has positive intrinsic value and it will be exercised by the option holder. The call has to be sufficiently deep in the money in order for the payoff to cover the purchase price of the option. The payoff schedule for the buyer has a slope of 1 for all in the money call options and there is no upper limit on the amount that can be collected when the option is exercised.

FIGURE 2-A-4: Payoff for the Buyer of a Call Option

Figure 2-A-5 shows the payoff diagram for the writer/seller of the call option. When the futures price is below the strike price the call option will not be exercised by the holder of the option, in which case the seller's net gain is the price paid by the holder and received by the seller. When the futures price is above the strike price the option will be exercised and the seller of the option is required to pay the holder the difference between the futures price and the strike price. In this region the slope of the payoff function is -1 and there is no upper limit on the size of the payment from the seller to the buyer of the option. If the call option is only slightly in the money then the payout made by the seller will be less than the premium received by the seller, in which case the seller earns a positive net profit on the transaction.

FIGURE 2-A-5: Payoff for the Seller of a Call Option

Figure 2-A-6 is the payoff diagram for the holder of a put option and Figure 2-A-7 is the payoff diagram for the writer of a put option. Notice that these two diagrams are mirror images of Figures 2-A-4 and 2-A-5. Specifically, the put option is not exercised and the price of the option is transferred from the holder of the option to the seller if the futures price is above the strike price. If the futures price is below the strike price then the put option is exercised and the payoff function has a slope of -1 for the option holder and 1 for the option seller. This means that below the strike price the seller transfers to the option holder the full change in the value of the commodity.

FIGURE 2-A-6: Payoff for the Buyer of a Put Option

FIGURE 2-A-7: Payoff for the Seller of a Put Option

The payoff diagram in Figure 2-A-8 is for a straddle A straddle is created by purchasing one call option and one put option with the same expiry date and the same strike price. With this configuration, either the call component or the put component has intrinsic value at all points in time and thus one of these components will be exercised on or before the straddle's expiry date. Figure 2-A-8 shows that the net payoff after accounting for the cost of constructing the straddle is positive provided that the futures price moves either sufficiently above or sufficiently below the strike price. As compared to buying an individual call or put, the cost of constructing a straddle position is relatively large. Consequently, a relatively large change in the futures price is required in order for the payout of the straddle to be large enough to cover the cost of constructing the straddle. A straddle is commonly held by traders who are speculating that increased future volatility in the futures price will result in a positive payout from either the call or the put component of the straddle.

FIGURE 2-A-8: Payoff for the Holder of a Straddle

The payoff diagram in Figure 2-A-9 is for a strangle. A strangle is the same as a straddle except now the strike price of the call is greater than the strike price of the put. This difference in strike price creates a zone for the futures price where both the call and put component of the strangle has zero intrinsic value. Strangles are popular amongst investors because they are less expensive to construct as compared to a straddle. This disadvantage is that a larger decrease or a larger increase in the futures price is required in order for a positive payoff to be generated. Constructing an inexpensive strangle by setting the strike price of the call well above the strike price of the put is a popular way to take a long shot position with the hope that an unforeseen event will cause either a large upward or downward surge in the futures price.

FIGURE 2-A-9: Payoff for the Holder of a Strangle

The last payoff diagram to be examined is for a butterfly spread, as illustrated in Figure 2-A-10. A butterfly spread is constructed by simultaneously buying and selling calls and/or put options with different strike prices. Describing the mechanics of a butterfly spread is beyond the scope of this course. What is important to note is that the butterfly spread has limited profit potential and for this reason is relatively inexpensive to construct. Specifically, as Figure 2-A-10 shows the net payoff for the butterfly spread is positive provided that increases or decreases in the futures price are not excessively large. The butterfly spread is a good example of the large number of complex payoff schedules that can be created by combining purchased and sold call and put options with different strike prices. An important limitation of the butterfly spread and other exotic option combinations is that the commission charge for combining the various components can quickly become excessive relative to the size of the expected payoff.

(iii) Option Pricing Sensitivity Analysis

A call or put option is different than a commodity futures contract in that the price of these instruments do not reflect fundamentals about supply and demand beyond what is already reflected in the futures price. In other words, if traders knew the stochastic process which governs how the futures price changes over time then all traders would agree on the same price for the option contract and as such their individual beliefs about the supply and demand fundamentals would not be relevant for the pricing of the option. A popular assumption is that traders agree that futures prices roughly follow a log normal distribution, in which case the Black (1976) option pricing formula can be used to price the option (the Black 1976 formula is often referred to generically as the Black-Scholes formula). One of the parameters which feed into the Black-Scholes formula is the future volatility of the log normal price distribution. Traders typically disagree on the level of future volatility, and this disagreement allows for active centralized trading in options contracts.

The Black (1976) option pricing formula and other pricing formulas that are similar to Black-Scholes have four important parameters: (1) the current futures price; (2) the option contract's strike price; (3) the number of days until the option contract expires; and (4) the volatility of the underlying futures price. A change in the strike price and an opposite change in the current futures price have similar impacts on the price of the option because the payoff depends on the difference between the futures price and the strike price. For this reason during comparative static analysis it is common to leave the strike price fixed and then focus on how variations in the current futures price affect the price of the option.

Of interest in this section is how the equilibrium price of the option, either when it is initially written or when it is subsequently exchanged between traders, is theoretically impacted by changes in the underlying futures price, the expiry date of the contract and futures price volatility. These comparative static results, which are derived using the Black (1976) model, are uniquely identified by a Greek letter. Consequently, the full set of comparative static outcomes are referred to as the "Greeks"

Before presenting the calculated values for the Greeks, it is useful to first describe the comparative static results at a more intuitive level. This is accomplished by examining the expected payoff of a European call option through use of a small scale Monte Carlo analysis. Due to discounting and other factors, the comparative static results concerning the expected payoff cannot directly be used to infer the comparative static results concerning the price of the option. Nevertheless, the expected payoff and the price of the option generally move in the same direction when impacted by a shock and for this reason initially focusing on the expected payoff is reasonable.

Simulated Payoffs for a Call Option

The simulation begins on September 15, 2019 and ends when the futures contract and call option both expire at the end of December of 2021. With normal expectations, the futures price is not expected to systematically increase or decrease over time. Assume that on September 15, 2019 the market expects the December, 2021 futures price to equal $4.00/bu. Moving forward from September 15th, the futures price is assumed to follow a geometric random walk without drift. Specifically, the percentage change in the futures price between period t and period t + 1 is assumed to be a normally distributed random variable with mean 0 and standard deviation σ*. Prices are generated on a weekly basis which implies that σ* = (1/52)0.5σ, where σ is a measure of annualized volatility for the price series. The next paragraph describes how existing futures price data should be used to estimate historic annualized volatility

If the data is daily then the first step when calculating annualized volatility is to take the natural log of the daily price and then create a new series consisting of the first difference of the logged prices. You should convince yourself with a simple example in an Excel worksheet that ln(Pt+1) - ln(Pt) is approximately equal to the percentage change in price: (Pt+1 - Pt)/Pt. The next step is to calculate the standard deviation of the ln(Pt+1) - ln(Pt) data series. Finally, multiply this standard deviation result by the square root of 252 to obtain annualized volatility. The 252 value is approximately equal to the number of trading days in a year. The method described here assumes that volatility remains constant over time. If this is not the case then more sophisticated methods such as ARCH or GARCH are use to estimate volatility

Figure 2-A-11 shows estimates of annualized volatility for CBOT corn futures spanning the years 1992 to 2016. This figure comes from a 2017 working paper titled "Future volatility forecast in agricultural commodity markets". It can be accessed here. Notice that volatility varies over the course of the marketing year, generally highest just prior to harvest and lowest just after harvest. Also notice that corn prices are significantly more volatile in the second half of the sample (2006 - 2016) as compared to the first half (1992 - 2005).

Properties of Simulated Payoffs for Call Option

Figures 2-A-12 and 2-A-13 illustrate three important results concerning how the expected payoff of the call option is impacted by the parameters which define the call (the expected payoff is the straight average of the 10 simulated payoffs). Each are discussed in turn.

Futures - Strike Wedge: Let Z equal the Futures - Strike wedge, which is defined as the initial (September, 2019) futures price minus the strike price. For a call option, a positive (negative) value for Z implies that the call option is initially in the money (out of the money). Figure 2-A-12 shows that a larger value for Z implies a higher expected payoff for the call option. For example, suppose the initial futures price is held fixed at $4.00/bu and the strike price is reduced by $0.05. In this case Z increases by $0.05/bu. The lower strike price relative to the initial futures price results in both more of the simulated price series ending up in the money, and a greater gap between the futures price and the strike price for those price series which do end up in the money. Thus, a larger value for Z necessarily implies a higher expected payoff for the call option.

A less obvious result concerns how the impact of a higher Z on the expected payoff depends on the size of Z. For the call option that is initially in the money (i.e., the bottom dashed line labeled SI), an increase in Z by $0.05/bu will increase the average payoff for the call option by an amount that is close to $0.05/bu. This is because as Figure 2-A-12 shows that there is a strong likelihood that a call option which begins highly in the money will remain in the money. When an option is expected to largely remain in the money then a $0.05/bu increase in Z will translate into a largely $0.05/bu increase in the expected payoff. In contrast, for the call option that is initially out of the money (i.e., the upper dashed line labeled SI), the increase in Z by $0.05/bu will increase the expected payoff of the call option by a comparatively small amount. This result emerges because the odds are relatively low that the call option will expire in the money. If the call option does not expire in the money even with the $0.05/bu increase in Z, then this increase will have had no impact on the call's payoff.


FIGURE 2-A-13: Simulated Corn Price with Low Volatility

Annualized Volatility: A comparison of the high volatility price series in Figure 2-A-12 and the low volatility price series in Figure 2-A-13 reveals how differences in the annualized volatility of the futures price affects the payoff of the call option. In both cases 3 out of the 10 simulated prices result in the expiring call being in the money. However, for the calls that do finish in the money, they tend to be much deeper in the money for the high volatility case (Figure 2-A-12) as compared to the low volatility case (Figure 2-A-13). Consequently, the payoff of the call option is expected to be higher with the higher level of price volatility. A more general interpretation of this result is that if the upside potential gain from holding the option is unlimited and the maximum downside potential loss from holding the option is the initial cost of the option, then large price swings give rise to higher gains without the corresponding offsetting losses. As was previously noted, the differences in opinion across speculators about the level of volatility is a key reason for active trading in centralized markets for option contracts.

Time Horizon: The third important comparative static result concerns how a longer time horizon affecs the expected payoff of the call option. Unlike the first two results, this property is difficult to observe in Figures 2-A-12 and 2-A-13. Consider instead Figure 2-A-14, which illustrates a simple additive binomial model of option pricing. With this model Pt+1 is equal to Pt + M with probability 0.5 and is equal to Pt - M with probability 0.5. With this assumption, n periods after the start of the price series, the price depends only on the total number of periods that price increased and not on the distribution of price increases and price decreases. As such, the probability of ending up at a particular pricing outcome n periods after the start of the price series is given by the binomial formula.

Suppose in Figure 2-A-14 the call option expires at time T1, which is 2 periods after the start. Using the binomial formula, the probability of obtaining price P3
3 is equal to 0.125. The payoff to the holder of the call if this price emerges is 2M. Thus, the expected payoff when the call expires at time T1 is 0.25M. Now suppose the option expires at time T2, which is 4 periods after the start. Once again using the binomial formula, the probability of ending up with price P5
5 is equal to 0.03125, and the probability of ending up with price P5
4 is equal to 0.15625. In this case the expected payoff from the call is equal to (0.03125*4 + 0.15625*2)M, which reduces to 0.4375M. The expected payoff with a T2 expiry is therefore significantly larger than the expected payoff with a T1 expiry. The intuition for this result is similar to the intuition for the previous result concerning price volatility. In this case, with no limit on the upside and the maximum loss equal to the purchase price of the option the longer is the time horizon before expiry the greater the potential for a large payoff without the potential for an equal-sized offsetting loss.

The Greeks

The comparative static results that were illustrated in the previous section can be generalized by examining the theoretical relationship between the price of an option and the three parameters which define the option. The formal comparative static results concerning the option's price are labelled delta for the marginal impact of a higher futures price, vega for the marginal impact of higher volatility and theta for the marginal impact of a longer time until the option expires. These three marginal impacts, together with two additional marginal impacts which are not included in this analysis, are referred to as the Greeks.

The "Greeks" are a collection of individual variables which measure the sensitivity of the option's price to the parameters of the pricing formula: delta (underlying futures price), vega (futures price volatility) and theta (time until expiry).

Marginal Versus Average Impacts

The sign of the marginal impacts which are measured by the Greeks also identify the sign of the average impacts. For example, a marginally higher value of the underlying futures price results in a marginally higher value of the call option when delta takes on a positive value. This positive relationship can also be observed on average, which means that higher values of the underlying futures prices correspond to higher call prices overall. In what follow both marginal and average values are examined.

Numerical values for the three Greek variables and a set of option prices which can be used to examine the average impacts are obtained from an option price calculator. The calculator can be built in an Excel worksheet using the mathematical equations which define the option prices and the "Greeks". These equations can be found in advanced textbooks or in the following June, 2013 blog post by Glyn Holton. You will be asked to download an option price calculator that was built by Professor Vercammen for use in Assignment 3.

The CME website provides a Black (1976) option pricing calculator that allows the Greeks to be calculated along with the theoretical price of the option. The Option's Delta

An option's delta is a measure of how the price of the option responds to marginal changes in the underlying futures price. The option pricing impact from an increase in the initial futures price holding the strike price constant is similar, but not identical, to the option pricing impact from a decrease in the strike price holding the initial futures price constant. Thus, similar to the discussion concerning Figure 2-A-12, if the underlying futures price is relatively high such that the call is deep in the money then we should expect the price of the call option to move almost one-for-one with a marginal increase in the initial futures price. Conversely, if the underlying futures price is relatively low such that he call is deep out of the money then we should expect the price of the call to move very little with a marginal increase in the initial futures price. Similar types of changes should be expected when examining average impacts.

The bottom half of Figure 2-A-16 reports the call option delta values for various combinations of the initial futures price and price volatility. As expected, the values are positive, and higher values of the underlying futures price correspond to higher values for delta. For example, when the expiry time is T = 40 weeks and the volatility is 0.3, the delta is 42 with a futures price of 390 and is 62 with a futures price of 410. This means that if the initial futures price increases by $0.01/bu from 390 to 391, then the price of the call will increase by $0.0042/bu. In contrast, with the same increase but starting from an initial futures price equal to 410, the price of the call will increase by $0.0062/bu.

The distribution of call prices which are reported in the top half of Figure 2-A-16 are consistent with the properties of the delta values. In other words, the average impact of a change in the underlying futures price is qualitatively similar to the marginal impact. These average impacts are quite stable. For example, an increase in the underlying futures price from 390 to 410 results in an increase in the price of the call by approximately 10 units for the four alternative time horizons and the three alternative volatility settings.

The Option's Vega

The bottom of Figure 2-A-17 shows vega, which is how the price of the call is impacted by marginally higher volatility. For example, with an expiry date of T = 40 and a call option that is at the money (i.e., the futures price is 400), increasing volatility from 30 to 31 will increase the price of the call by 0.526, starting from a price of 15.81. Figure 2-A-17 shows that a marginal increase in volatility has its biggest impact on the price of the call when the call is at the money versus being either deep in or deep out of the money. As well, the vega value is larger for option contracts with longer time horizons. Notice that the size of vega changes very little in response to changes the level of volatility.

The prices in the top of Figure 2-A-17, which can be used to examine average impacts, are consistent with the marginal impacts that are measured by Vega. Notice that the increase in the price of the call in response to higher futures price volatility (i.e., moving down a column) are relatively large. This outcome is expected given the results from the previous section where it was shown that simulated payoffs from a call option are much higher with higher levels of futures price volatility. Notice also from Figure 2-A-17 that the impact of higher volatility on the price of the call is larger for longer time horizons.

The Option's Theta

The theta values in the bottom half of Figure 2-A-18 show how the price of the option changes with a one day reduction in the option's expiry time. The negative values imply that the time value of the option declines with a marginally shorter time until the expiry date.
