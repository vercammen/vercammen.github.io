Module 1.B focused on a generic market that treated futures and spot prices as equivalent. In real world markets futures and spot prices track each other but not perfectly. Understanding the relationship between futures and spot prices is crucial for understanding price risk management (i.e., hedging), which is the topic of Module 1.D. In the first section of this module the theoretical link between futures and spot prices are examined. The middle section lays out the assumptions which are used to incorporate futures trading and regional spot markets into the simulation model that was constructed in Module 1.B. The final section of this module presents the simulation results, which illustrate how futures and spot prices are connected over time and space.

(i) Theoretical Concepts

The two most important theoretical relationships which describe the link between futures and spot prices are the basis and the price expectations which define the forward curve. Each of these are discussed in turn.

Basis Defined

The main link between futures prices and spot prices is the basis. The basis is defined as the spot price minus the futures price. Each spot market has its own basis, and it is necessary to define the futures contract (e.g., December, 2019) when specifying a value for the basis. The value of the basis varies over space because the spot price in a particular region depends on the cost of transporting the commodity to market. The value of the basis varies over time because as time progresses the cost of carrying the commodity until the expiry of the futures contract changes.

Suppose the basis is measured at time t in spot market X using a futures contract that expires at time T and has Chicago as its delivery option. The basis in this market consists of an adjustment for location minus the cost of carrying the commodity from time t until time T. The adjustment for location is the spot price in region X minus the spot price in Chicago. This spot price difference will vary over time with a seasonal component and a stationary random component. The simplest case is when the commodity is shipped from region X to Chicago for export because in this case the adjustment for location is the cost of transporting the commodity from region X to Chicago multiplied by -1.


Basis = Spot Price - Futures Price

        = Adjustment for Location - Carrying Cost

For agricultural commodities such as corn for which the futures contract has Chicago as the delivery location the basis in the Chicago spot market is equal to the carrying cost. Moreover, as the futures contract approaches its expiry date, the carrying cost approaches zero, which means that for the Chicago market the spot price and futures price converge as the expiry of the contract approaches.


Convergence Propery: The Chicago spot price and the price of a futures contract that is about to expire should be approximately equal.

The Convergence property is very important because it provides a precise connection between futures and spot prices. Suppose the futures price was above the Chicago spot price when the futures contract was expiring. Riskless arbitrage profits could be earned if an agent takes a short position in the futures market, purchases the commodity in the Chicago spot market and then delivers the commodity to fulfill the requirements of the short futures position. If instead the futures price was below the Chicago spot price, then the agent would take a long futures position, accept delivery of the commodity to fulfill the requirements of the long position and then sell the commodity in the spot price. This type of arbitrage activity would cause the futures and spot prices to adjust until convergence is achieved and arbitrage profits vanish.

The component of the basis that accounts for the spot market location depends on whether or not there is a transportation link between the spot market and the Chicago market. If there is a transportation link then the adjustment is simply the transportation cost between the spot market and the Chicago market. In this module we consider the more complex case where there is no transportation link between the spot market and Chicago. Specifically, we assume that the commodity is exported through the U.S. Gulf Coast, in which case the adjustment for location accounts for both the cost of transporting the commodity to the export location and the difference in the spot prices at the Gulf Coast and Chicago.


Basis = Adjustment for Location - Carrying Cost

Case 1: Transportation Link Between Spot Market and Chicago

  • Adjustment for Location = -(Transportation Cost Between Local Market and Chicago)

    • Case 2: Transportation Link Between Spot Market and Gulf Coast

  • Adjustment for Location = (Gulf Coast Spot Price - Chicago Spot Price) - (Transportation Cost from Spot Market to Gulf Coast)

    Price Expectations and the Forward Curve

    The second major link between futures prices and spot prices is price expectations and the forward curve. Prior to a futures contract expiring riskless arbitrage between the futures price and the spot price is not possible and so it is necessary for traders to form expectations about future spot prices. The two general categories of price expectations are unbiased (also referred to as normal) and biased. With normal price expectations, the equilibrium futures price at time t is the expected Chicago spot price at time T where T is the time that the futures contract expires. Biased price expectations come in two flavours. A market for which the futures price is biased down (up) is referred to as normal backwardation (contango). Biased expectations will be examined in greater detail in Module 1-D in the context of hedging. For the rest of this module assume that price expectations are normal.


    Normal Price Expectations: Futures price at time t = Expected Chicago spot price when contract expires at time T

    Figure 1-C-1 illustrates normal price expectations since there is no change in the expected futures price over time.

    An important property of futures prices which has a strong connection to price expectations is the forward curve. This curve is the set of prices for all futures contracts that are trading at a given point in time. Table 1-C-3 shows the July 12, 2019 settle prices for the full set of CBOT corn contracts that span July, 2019 to July, 2021. The graph of these prices, which is the forward curve, is given by Figure 1-C-2. Normal price expectations implies that as of July 12, 2019, traders believed the Chicago spot price for corn in mid September when the September futures contract expires is $4.5425/bu. Similarly, traders believe the spot price for corn in mid December when the December futures contract expires is $4.5925/bu. And so forth.


    TABLE 1-C-3: July 12, 2019 CBOT Corn Futures Prices

    The slope of the forward curve reveals the spreads in neighboring futures prices. For example, the spread between the September, 2019 and December, 2019 corn futures is 4.5925 - 4.5425 = $0.05/bu. With normal price expectations this spread in the pair of futures prices is equal to the spread between the expected mid September, 2019 Chicago spot price and the expected mid December, 2019 Chicago spot price. In the previous module the change in the expected spot price over time was due to the carrying cost. Thus, with normal price expectations there is direct link between the slope of the forward curve and expected carrying costs in the spot market.


    With normal price expectations, the slope of the forward curve is a measure of expected carrying costs in the Chicago spot market.

If stockout considerations are excluded by focusing the the intra-harvest period then according to the theory from Module 1.B, the forward curve for storable agricultural commodities is expected to slope up due to positive carrying costs.     (ii) Modeling Assumptions

    The purpose of this section is to describe the assumptions which are required to incorporate a futures market and regional spot prices into the simulation model that was constructed in Module 1.B. Prior to laying out these assumptions it is useful to first discuss in greater detail the concept of price discovery in futures and spot markets.

    Price Discovery Revisited

    An important role of centralized trading of commodity futures is price discovery. With regional spot markets that have different prices due to differences in transportation costs to export markets it is important to ask which prices in particular are being discovered by a commodity futures market. In Module 1.B it was shown how arbitrage in the market for storage leads to the efficient allocation of stocks and consumption over time. In a similar fashion the active arbitrage of prices across regional markets results in a spatial pricing equilibrium within which stocks cannot be profitably shipped from one region to another. Traders recognize this arbitrage activity over time and space, which means that implicitly the full set of spot prices across time and space are continually being discovered by commodity futures markets.

    It is not feasible to report all of the prices that are discovered over time and space. Instead, prices for select futures contracts with varying expiry dates are reported for one specific location -- typically Chicago. Spot prices in the various regional markets are then derived by adding the basis which is specific to that region to the price of the next-to-expire futures contract. The size of the basis changes continually over time as part of the localized price discovery process. For example, in Table 1-C-1 the spot prices for corn in the southern Iowa and St. Louis markets are derived by adding the current value of the regional basis to the September, 2019 futures price for corn. The idea that spot prices are derived from futures prices rather than vice versa may be confusing because a futures price is often referred to as a derivative price (e.g., see the Investopedia entry for derivative pricing). This confusion can be set aside if one instead interprets the futures price as being derived from the market fundamentals which determine the full array of spot prices over time and space.

    Assumptions to Extend Simulation Model

    For the purpose of the simulation model, assume that traders in the futures market are discovering the current and expected future spot price of corn at the Gulf Coast export location. In other words, the set of equilibrium spot prices that were simulated in Module 1.B are assumed to be the set of prices that emerge in the Gulf Coast export market. This is a reasonable assumption because as previously noted the Gulf Coast by far has the largest export share in the U.S. corn market. Also assume that the Chicago spot price is equal to the Gulf Coast spot price minus a discount, which depends on the season and level of stocks in the market. This is a strong assumption because in reality the Chicago market and the Gulf Coast market are serving different overseas customers and so the price difference across the two locations will be much more complex. With the Chicago spot price now specified it is straight forward to specify the set of futures prices in the simulation model. Indeed, the convergence property and normal price expectations assumption ensures that the futures price is equal to the expected Chicago spot price when the futures contract expires.

    As shown in Module 1.D, hedging effectiveness and hedging profits depends on how the basis changes over time. For this reason the determinants of the basis are emphasized in the next module. In this module the basis is calculated for both the Gulf Coast and Kansas City, which is a typical midwestrn city in the corn growing region of the U.S. Midwest that exports through the Gulf Coast. Similar to the Chicago spot price, the Kansas City spot price is assumed to equal the Gulf Coast spot price minus a discount, which depends on the season and level of stocks in the market. This assumption is quite reasonable because according to theory the discount should equal the cost of transporting the corn from Kansas City to the Gulf Coast using a combination of rail freight and a barge down the Mississippi River.     Estimation of Price Discount Functions

    The purpose of this section is to estimate the Gulf Coast - Chicago and Gulf Coast - Kansas City price discounts for corn. The estimated price discounts are subtracted from the simulated Gulf Coast spot price in order to obtain simulated spot prices for Chicago and Kansas City. The Gulf Coast - Chicago price discount is an estimate of the average price difference across the two export markets. The Gulf Coast - Kansas City price discount is an estimate of the average cost of transporting corn from Kansas City to the Gulf Coast. Both discounts depend on the season and the level of seasonally-adjusted stocks.

    The results are shown in Table 1-C-4. The column on the left is the Gulf Coast - Chicago discount and the column on the right is the Gulf Coast - Kansas City price discount. In each case the dependent variable is the deflated average quarterly spot price of corn in the Gulf Coast minus the deflated average monthly price of corn in the regional spot market. The explanatory variables include dummy variables for the winter, spring and summer quarters, and a measure of seasonally-adjusted stocks. The dummy variable for the fall season is omitted which means that the coefficients on the three included seasonal dummies must be interpreted relative to the fall season. For both Chicago and Kansas City, the negative values for the coefficients of the winter, spring and summer dummies implies that the price discount is smaller in the winter, spring and summer than it is in the fall. This result is expected because regions the Midwest have surplus production region and so the spot price in these regions tend to be lowest when stocks are highest in the fall season. The positive value on the Stock Residual variable is also expected because it reveals that the price discount is largest when stocks in the midwestern region are above their long-term average.

    (iii) Simulation Results

    The purpose of this section is to graphically display the simulated futures and spot prices, the simulated basis for the Gulf Coast and Kansas City and the simulated forward curve. As previously noted, the simulated spot prices from Module 1.B are assumed to equal the spot prices that are observed in the Gulf Coast market. For a particular year and quarter, the Chicago spot price is derived by adding to the simulated Gulf Coast spot price the estimated Gulf Coast - Chicago price discount as per the left side regression in Table 1-C-4. Let F7 denote a futures contract that expires in Q7 (i.e., the third quarter of the second year). The F7 contract is priced using the expected Q7 Chicago spot price. The Gulf Coast spot price minus the F7 futures price gives the F7 basis for the Gulf Coast. The derivation of the Kansas City basis is analogous.

    Recall from the construction of the simulation model in Module 1-B that with a 0.5 probability the year 2 harvest will be reduced by an amount L. When L takes on a small value, the year 1 market was shown to stock out, and when L takes on a large value the year 1 market was shown to carry over from year 1 to year 2. The two alternative assumptions about the size of L, together with the two alternative markets for hedging (i.e., the Gulf Coast and Kansas City), imply that there are four possible scenarios to consider. Rather than presenting all four, the following combinations are featured. The first combination is the Gulf Coast market in a large loss (stock carry over) scenario, and the second combination is the Kansas City market in a small loss (stockout) scenario. The results for the two omitted scenarios are similar to the results for the two included scenarios.

    Figure 1-C-6 shows the simulation results for the Gulf Coast spot market. The solid bars represent Chicago prices and the light-shaded bars represent the Gulf Coast prices. The potential for a large loss in the year 2 harvest implies that stocks are carried forward through time, and the spot prices at both locations increase continually over the eight quarters. The columns for the first four quarters represent actual prices and the columns for the last four quarters represent expected prices from the perspective of a decision maker in Q1 through Q4 (recall that the harvest in Q5 is uncertain). The difference in the heights of the dark and light shaded columns is a measure of the price discounts which are given by regression results in the left column of Table 1-C-4. The price of the F7 futures contract, which is given by the dark horizontal line, is equal to expected Chicago spot price in Q7. The expected Gulf Coast basis is the vertical difference between the Gulf Coast spot price and the F7 futures price. Notice that the expected basis is negative for Q2 and positive for Q3, Q4 and Q6. Moreover, the basis is increasing over time because the expected futures price remains constant whereas the expected spot price is rising due to the carrying cost.

    The solid curved line in Figures 1-C-6 and 1-C-7 is the forward curve. In the Gulf Coast market the forward curve is strictly upward sloping due to the carry over of stocks. A market with an upward sloping forward curve is commonly referred to as normal carrying cost market. In the Kansas City market the stock out results in a downward sloping portion and a negative price spread within the forward curve. A market with a negative price spread is commonly referred to as an inverted market. As previously noted, seasonally produced crops such as corn commonly have price inversions when stocks from a new harvest are expected.


    Normal Carrying Cost Market: The forward curve is upward sloping.

    Inverted Market: The forward curve has a downward sloping segment (i.e., a negative price spread).
